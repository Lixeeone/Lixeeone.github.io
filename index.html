My research is guided by the principle of “AI4Science, Science2AI”: on the one hand, using artificial intelligence to accelerate scientific discovery; on the other hand, allowing scientific laws and mechanisms to inform the design and evolution of intelligent systems. Under this principle, I advocate revisiting and re-constructing “primitive biological structures” within computational models. In a strongly interdisciplinary setting, I aim to systematically integrate ideas from computer science, psychology, and cognitive neuroscience, and to use biological and cognitive mechanisms as inspiration for algorithmic architectures and learning paradigms. The overarching goal is to address key challenges in contemporary AI, such as catastrophic forgetting, long-horizon sequence modeling, and performance bottlenecks under realistic conditions.

During my undergraduate studies and early research experience, I have participated in several projects that focus on fundamental mechanisms and theory-driven modeling. I have taken or self-studied courses and literature in affective computing, human–computer interaction, natural language processing, and cognitive & computational neuroscience. These experiences have given me a relatively deep and integrated understanding of how these fields couple and intersect, and have also helped me develop an “off-the-beaten-path” style of problem decomposition and solution design. For example, when modeling emotional and cognitive processes, I am particularly interested in extracting structurally meaningful and dynamically relevant features from the human perception–representation–decision pipeline, and mapping them into differentiable model components or learning rules, so that biological plausibility and robustness can be jointly improved.

A central question that motivates my research is how to better inspire machines from humans, and how to better enable machines to serve humans. I hope that by building models that are grounded in real human behavior and neural mechanisms, machine learning can gradually move beyond pure data fitting towards more embodied, interpretable, and adaptive forms of intelligence. At the same time, I aim for future intelligent systems not only to achieve competitive performance on benchmarks, but also to provide long-term, trustworthy, and human-centered value in terms of safety, reliability, and seamless human–AI collaboration in scientific and societal applications.
